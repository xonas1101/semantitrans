SEMANTIC COMMUNICATION OVER A NOISY WIRELESS CHANNEL (semcom_eval.py)
=====================================================================
Plot: semcom_snr.png   Data: semcom_snr.csv   (n = 25 test clips)

Semantic communication = transmitting the MEANING of a message in the fewest
bits, instead of reproducing the exact signal bit-for-bit. We compare four
ways of sending an English utterance to a Hindi-speaking receiver across the
same BPSK channel, at channel SNRs from 10 dB (good) to -5 dB (very bad):

1. TRADITIONAL  — classical digital transmission: the waveform as 16-bit PCM
                  bits over BPSK (literature-standard baseline). Receiver
                  decodes the bits, runs ASR + translation. ~1,692,058 bits/msg
2. SEMANTIC     — sender runs ASR + idiom resolution, transmits only the
   (text bits)    meaning as UTF-8 text over BPSK, uncoded.     ~700 bits/msg
3. SEMANTIC     — same text protected by a rate-1/3 repetition code with
   (rep-3 coded)  majority-vote decoding (fair coded baseline). ~2,100 bits/msg
4. LEARNED      — OUR from-scratch semantic channel codec (transformer
   CODEC          encoder -> 16 symbols per token, 8-BIT QUANTIZED -> channel
                  -> transformer decoder), random-SNR training. ~2,309 bits/msg

Meaning preservation (chrF of received Hindi vs the scheme's clean-channel
output — i.e. pure channel robustness):

  SNR(dB)   BER        traditional   text bits   rep-3 coded   learned codec
    10      3.9e-06      0.95          1.00         1.00          0.57
     5      6.0e-03      0.18          0.61         1.00          0.38
     2      3.8e-02      0.13          0.14         0.74          0.28
     0      7.9e-02      0.05          0.03         0.31          0.16
    -2      1.3e-01      0.01          0.02         0.15          0.12
    -5      2.1e-01      0.01          0.01         0.02          0.10

TAKEAWAYS
- This is the literature-standard crossover (see literature_comparison.png):
  classical digital transmission is near-perfect on a good channel (0.95 at
  10 dB) and collapses below it; semantic schemes own everything below.
- On a good channel, text bits MATCH classical quality at 2418x fewer bits.
- Classical cliffs FIRST: a 1.7-Mbit message collects ~10,000 bit errors at
  5 dB where the 700-bit text message collects ~4.
- The rep-3 coded text (3x the bits, 806x compression) holds a perfect score
  down to 5 dB and dominates the mid-range — the practical sweet spot.
  Coding DELAYS the cliff, it does not remove it (0.02 at -5 dB).
- Our learned codec degrades GRACEFULLY with no cliff: only scheme still
  working at -5 dB (0.10). 8-bit quantization cost nothing (bits/msg
  9,236 -> 2,309).

RAYLEIGH FADING (semcom_snr_rayleigh.png — the realistic channel)
Quasi-static flat fading, perfect CSI, one fade per message. chrF:

  SNR(dB)   traditional   text bits   rep-3 coded   learned codec
    10        0.69          0.80         0.92          0.53
     5        0.28          0.51         0.69          0.33
     2        0.09          0.23         0.43          0.20
     0        0.06          0.09         0.27          0.14
    -2        0.04          0.08         0.20          0.10
    -5        0.02          0.02         0.15          0.11

- Deep fades hurt every digital scheme even at 10 dB average SNR.
- REP-3 CODED TEXT WINS AT EVERY SNR under fading — redundancy is exactly
  what survives a fade. The codec is closest behind at the bottom (0.11 at
  -5 dB vs the waveform's 0.02).

NON-IDIOMATIC SENTENCES, GOLD REFS (semcom_snr_gold_literal.png)
On literal-only sentences traditional TIES semantic text at 10 dB (0.42 vs
0.43 — same models either side of a clean channel). The gap on the full test
set is idiom mistranslation, not channel noise. Semantic's advantage on plain
sentences is bandwidth (2418x) and low-SNR survival, not quality.

BANDWIDTH EFFICIENCY (semcom_efficiency.png)
At 10 dB, where quality is ~equal, meaning-per-kilobit: text 1.43 chrF/kbit,
rep-3 0.48, codec 0.25, traditional 0.0006 — a ~2,500x efficiency gap.

WER VIEW (semcom_wer*.png): WER of received Hindi, same runs. Text schemes
below their cliff decode to garbage LONGER than the reference (uncoded 2.6 at
0 dB; rep-3 6.0 at -5 dB), so WER>1 is a hallucination-length artifact —
chrF (bounded) is the primary metric.

SEMANTIC NOISE (semcom_semnoise.png / .csv): corrupt each sender-side word
with prob p on a CLEAN channel. Text chrF 1.0 -> 0.43 as p goes 0 -> 0.3;
codec falls FASTER (1.0 -> 0.26). Honest negative result: the codec fights
channel noise, not meaning corruption — semantic noise must be fixed at the
source (better ASR/idiom resolution), nothing downstream can undo it.

CAVEATS (say these if asked)
- Traditional is digital but UNCODED PCM; with a modern LDPC/turbo code it
  would hold to ~5 dB before its cliff (and cost more bits). rep-3 on text is
  similarly a simple code. The crossover shape is the standard result either
  way.
- Curves are scored against each scheme's own clean output (robustness, the
  literature-standard view). Gold-referenced runs (semcom_snr_gold*.csv) cap
  every scheme at the translator's quality (~0.35) — use them for ordering,
  not cross-paper comparison.
- Rayleigh model is quasi-static flat fading with perfect CSI; no Doppler /
  frequency selectivity. Interleaving would help rep-3 across fades.
